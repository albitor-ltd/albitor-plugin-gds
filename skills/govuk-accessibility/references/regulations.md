# Public Sector Bodies Accessibility Regulations 2018

> **Sources:** [GOV.UK: Understanding accessibility requirements for public sector bodies](https://www.gov.uk/guidance/accessibility-requirements-for-public-sector-websites-and-apps) and [GDS: Understanding WCAG](https://www.gov.uk/service-manual/helping-people-to-use-your-service/understanding-wcag).

## What the regulations are

The **Public Sector Bodies (Websites and Mobile Applications) (No. 2) Accessibility Regulations 2018** require public sector bodies to make their websites and mobile apps accessible, and to publish an accessibility statement. The technical standard is **WCAG 2.2 Level AA**.

## Who they apply to

- Central government and local government organisations.
- Some **publicly funded charities and other non-government organisations**, where they:
  - are mostly publicly funded, **and/or**
  - provide services essential to the public, **and/or**
  - are aimed at disabled people.

**Excluded / fully exempt:**

- Non-government organisations (most charities) that are **not** mostly publicly funded, **not** providing essential public services, and **not** aimed at disabled people.
- **Public sector broadcasters** and their subsidiaries.
- **Schools and nurseries** — *partially exempt*: only content needed to use their services (e.g. a form to register absence, or a school-meals booking system) must be accessible.

## The requirement

Make websites and mobile apps **perceivable, operable, understandable and robust** by meeting **WCAG 2.2 level AA** (every Level A and AA success criterion), and publish (and keep updated) an **accessibility statement**.

## Key dates

| Date | What applied |
| --- | --- |
| **23 September 2018** | Regulations came into force. |
| **23 September 2019** | All public sector websites published **on or after 23 September 2018** had to comply. |
| **23 September 2020** | All public sector websites (including older ones) had to comply. |
| **23 June 2021** | All public sector **mobile applications** had to comply. |

> The technical standard referenced has since been updated to **WCAG 2.2** (from WCAG 2.1); the GOV.UK guidance and Service Manual now require WCAG 2.2 AA as the minimum.

## Partial exemptions — content that need not meet the standard

These are *content* exemptions; the rest of the site still must comply, and you must **list them in your accessibility statement**.

- **Disproportionate burden** — you may claim that fixing specific content would impose an excessive burden, but only after a **documented assessment** weighing the cost/effort against your organisation's size and resources and the benefit to disabled users. It is not a blanket get-out and must be reassessed periodically.
- **Third-party content** that you do not fund, develop, or control (e.g. an embedded external widget).
- **Pre-recorded time-based media** (audio/video) published **before 23 September 2020**.
- **Live** time-based media (live audio/video).
- **Office-file documents** (PDF, Word, etc.) published **before 23 September 2018**, unless needed to use an active service.
- **Online maps**, provided essential information is given in an accessible alternative (e.g. a postal address).
- **Heritage collection items** that can't practically be made accessible.
- **Archived websites** not needed for active services and not updated.

> Even where content is exempt under these regulations, you should still provide an **accessible alternative** where reasonably possible (see Equality Act below).

## Accessibility statement obligation

Every in-scope body must **publish an accessibility statement** in a specified format and keep it up to date. See `accessibility-statement.md` for the required content and a template. Statements should be reviewed regularly (at least annually) and whenever the service changes materially.

## Monitoring

- The **Government Digital Service (GDS)** monitors compliance **on behalf of the Cabinet Office (the Minister for the Cabinet Office)**.
- GDS tests a **sample** of public sector websites and apps each year, checks accessibility statements, and can ask organisations to fix problems.

## Enforcement

- **Equality and Human Rights Commission (EHRC)** enforces in **England, Scotland and Wales**.
- **Equality Commission for Northern Ireland (ECNI)** enforces in **Northern Ireland**.
- They can take action including investigations and court proceedings for failure to comply or to publish/correct a statement.
- Individuals report problems and seek support through the **Equality Advisory and Support Service (EASS)** (Great Britain), or **ECNI** in Northern Ireland.

## Relationship to the Equality Act 2010

The accessibility regulations sit **alongside** the **Equality Act 2010** (and the **Disability Discrimination Act 1995** in Northern Ireland). The Equality Act independently requires service providers to make **reasonable adjustments** for disabled people and not to discriminate. This duty applies **regardless** of any exemption under the accessibility regulations — so a "disproportionate burden" claim under the regulations does not remove your Equality Act obligation to provide reasonable adjustments and accessible alternatives.
